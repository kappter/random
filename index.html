Benford's Law Analysis

Also known as the First-Digit Law

Analyzing...

In 1938, physicist Frank Benford discovered that in many naturally occurring datasets — from river lengths to population figures to financial transactions — the leading digit is not uniformly distributed. Instead, smaller digits appear far more often: digit 1 leads ~30% of the time, digit 2 about 17.6%, and so on down to digit 9 at just 4.6%.

This follows a precise logarithmic law: P(d) = log₁₀(1 + 1/d). The law applies to data that spans several orders of magnitude and arises naturally from multiplicative processes. It is widely used in forensic accounting to detect fraud — fabricated numbers tend to deviate from Benford's distribution because humans unconsciously over-use certain digits.

For file byte analysis, bytes that represent natural data (text, measurements, prices) may conform, while encrypted or compressed files — which are designed to be uniform — will not conform. This makes Benford conformance a useful proxy for file entropy and authenticity.

Ulam Spiral — Digit Grid

Each cell = one generated digit, placed in a spiral from center outward. Colors reveal patterns in the sequence.

🌀

What is the Ulam Spiral?

A visual fingerprint of any digit sequence

The Ulam Spiral was invented by mathematician Stanislaw Ulam in 1963 while doodling during a boring conference. He arranged integers in a spiral and noticed that prime numbers cluster along diagonal lines — a pattern that still lacks a complete mathematical explanation.

Here, we adapt the concept as a digit sequence visualizer: each generated digit is placed as a colored cell in a square grid, filling from the center outward in a clockwise spiral. The result is a unique visual fingerprint of the algorithm. A truly random sequence produces a uniformly mixed mosaic with no visible structure. Biased or patterned sequences reveal themselves as color clusters, stripes, or diagonal bands.

The center cell (marked with a border) is always the very first digit generated. Each ring outward represents the next layer of the spiral. With enough digits, you can visually compare whether different algorithms produce genuinely different-looking patterns — or suspiciously similar ones.

What is the Consecutive Pairs Heatmap?

A serial correlation detector — exposing hidden structure in digit sequences

Each cell at row i, column j shows how often digit i was immediately followed by digit j in the generated sequence. This is called a lag-1 transition matrix — a core tool in statistical randomness testing.

In a truly random sequence, every pair (i→j) should occur with roughly equal frequency, producing a uniform, flat heatmap with no visible hot-spots. Biased or structured PRNGs reveal themselves as bright diagonal bands, corner clusters, or stripes. The infamous RANDU algorithm is a perfect example — its consecutive pairs collapse onto just 15 diagonal lines, visible immediately in this view.

Cell color intensity encodes frequency: bright/hot = frequent pair, dark/cool = rare pair. Hover over any cell to see the exact count and percentage. The diagonal (i→i) shows how often the same digit repeats consecutively — in a fair sequence, this should match all other cells.
